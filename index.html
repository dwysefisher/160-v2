160 years later: Eureka College continues to transform lives

by Dr. Junius Rodriguez, Eureka College Professor of History

The pioneer families who settled this community valued and celebrated the human spirit. One local historian stated, “Their chief aim was not great wealth but civilization. So, while they built their homes they helped build school houses and churches, sometimes living in dwellings lacking conveniences so that they might provide the other necessities of civilization.” They recognized education as the means to an end, thus preparing their sons and daughters alike for a world that was yet to be created.

In the egalitarian spirit of the frontier, reform-minded founders of the community of Walnut Grove established institutions of higher education to advance the civic impulse of abolitionism, the incipient cause of woman’s rights, and the social gospel calling to minister to the less fortunate through missionary efforts. The timeless values of the founders still animate the institutions that they helped to fashion.

Walnut Grove Seminary (1848) and Walnut Grove Academy (1851) were the academic predecessors of Eureka College, which was chartered by the Illinois Assembly on February 6, 1855. PULL QUOTE: As the first school in Illinois (and the third in the nation) to educate women on an equal basis with men, the new institution embodied a revolutionary ethic. The list of eminent visitors to the College—including Abraham Lincoln, Horace Mann, Susan B. Anthony, and Booker T. Washington—is a testimony to the power of such ideas and values to transform society.

As a church-related liberal arts college affiliated with the Christian Church (Disciples of Christ), Eureka College prepared students for lives of service and leadership within the larger community. Many of its earliest graduates found their respective calling in the fields of education and the ministry with more than one hundred graduates participating in the important work of the foreign missions. The school provided educational leaders as well with over forty alumni eventually serving as college or university presidents across the nation. In time, Eureka College produced leaders in national social endeavors like the movements for temperance and for woman suffrage. Political leaders would also emerge as Eureka College boasts among its alumni nine members of Congress, three governors, and the fortieth president of the United States, Ronald W. Reagan (Class of 1932).

Eureka College has always demonstrated a mission-specific resiliency to cultivate excellence in the areas of learning, service, and leadership and it has done so for 160 years since its chartering. The staying power of the institution (only 267 of the nation’s more than 3,000 colleges and universities are older than Eureka College) is testimony to the shared appreciation of what the school represents and the value-added benefit of what it provides to the larger community.

The truest meaning of Eureka College might best be described in words often used by Dean Samuel G. Harrod, a legendary figure in the life of the College, who often said “Eureka College: Many larger, few smaller, none better!” The pioneer settlers who envisioned this institution over a century-and-a-half ago decided to plant the new College near the life-giving spring that nurtured the community and provided the social gathering place of the rustic settlement. Their decision could not have been more apt. This small college has impacted the nation and the world; the spring of knowledge continues to nurture the many who gather here in this special place “’neath the elms.”
